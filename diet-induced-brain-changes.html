Diet-Induced Obesity and the Braking System of the Brain

Published on: May 01, 2023

Introduction

Imagine a switch in your brain that helps control your eating habits - a brake of sorts that tells you when you've had enough. Now, imagine that this switch isn't functioning as it should. A fascinating study from the University of Calgary brings light to this very situation, revealing a compelling connection between diet-induced obesity and alterations in brain function. This ground-breaking research is paving the way for a deeper understanding of the obesity epidemic, which has profound implications for our overall health.

The Discovery

The detailed research findings, recently published in Nature Neuroscience, uncovers an alarming reduction in the brain's brake signal within the lateral orbitofrontal cortex in obese mice. The orbitofrontal cortex - the region that plays a crucial role in reward-based decision making - appeared to be directly impacted by diet-induced obesity. The revelation that obesity could potentially disrupt the functionality of this critical region of the brain is indeed a disturbing realization.

Deep Dive Into the Study

Utilizing mice models, researchers embarked on an in-depth exploration of the changes in goal-directed behavior in relation to obesity. The experiment involved manipulating the brain's brake signal in the lateral orbitofrontal cortex of lean mice. The results were nothing short of startling. Even after consuming enough sucrose to satisfy their hunger, mice persisted in their consumption - almost as if they were driven by an insatiable desire implanted in their brains.

"Remarkably, mice continued eating sugar even when their physical needs were fulfilled!"

The Reversal

Perhaps the most encouraging aspect of the study was the discovery that it was possible to restore normal activity within the orbitofrontal cortex of obese mice. With this adjustment, the mice regained the ability to control their eating habits, demonstrating a reduction in their preference for sucrose once their hunger was satisfied. This suggests that the brain changes related to diet-induced obesity might not be permanent, which offers hope for potential treatments.

Implications for Humans

While the study primarily focused on mice, the findings may have significant implications for humans. Given the physiological similarities between humans and mice, it's plausible that similar mechanisms may be at play in human brains. As such, the research team is currently spearheading a pilot study to delve into the possible effects of these findings on human subjects. This pioneering research could potentially herald a paradigm shift in our understanding of obesity, highlighting novel strategies to combat this global health issue.

Reference: "Disinhibition of the orbitofrontal cortex biases decision-making in obesity" by Stephanie Borgland et al., Nature Neuroscience. (Closed Access)
