Unleash Your Brain's Potential: The Neuroscientist's Guide to Learning

Published on: May 23, 2023

Introduction

Imagine, if you will, a skill that we as humans possess which, like the master key of a grand old mansion, has the potential to unlock countless doors. This unparalleled power we hold is our ability to learn. But, how can we refine this master key, molding it into the perfect instrument to open the doors of knowledge more proficiently for students?

The Brain's Filing System

Our brain, like a sophisticated filing system, neatly tucks away two major types of memories. Declarative memories are akin to the specific documents you keep in this system — your address, your phone number, your go-to sushi place. You intentionally remember these facts, pulling them out when necessary. Then we have implicit memories, which behave more like the automatic, smooth mechanisms that work behind the scenes. These are the skills you've honed so well that they come naturally, like pedaling a bike or playing a familiar tune on your guitar.

But the files in our cognitive filing system aren't all alike. Declarative memories can be further subdivided into episodic and semantic memories. Episodic memories are like home videos, containing snippets of personal events. Semantic memories, on the other hand, are the plain, unadorned facts of your life. They are like the encyclopedic entries that give you context-free knowledge.

Now, let's consider two operations that the brain performs with these files: recognition and recall. Recognition is the warm feeling of familiarity when you come across something you've seen or heard before. It's like identifying a melody without knowing the song title or lyrics. Recall, on the other hand, is like singing the song, knowing the lyrics, the tune, and even the band's name. It's far more demanding and requires the mental effort of retrieving details.

To help you mold that master key, here are four neuroscience-informed strategies:

Amplify Your Recall Power: The Science-Backed Approach

Visualize your brain as a bustling train station, with each memory as a train arriving at and departing from the platform. The act of recalling information is akin to successfully navigating this bustling station to find the correct train — a specific memory. Each successful recall strengthens the mental 'route' to that particular train, enabling you to find it more readily the next time. This process mirrors the well-documented phenomenon in neuroscience known as Hebb's rule, succinctly captured in the phrase "neurons that fire together, wire together".

Enter self-testing, a powerful tool for enhancing recall. Grounded in the principles of active retrieval, self-testing functions as an efficient stationmaster, ensuring the smooth operation of this bustling memory station.

Numerous scientific studies underline the efficacy of self-testing. One prominent example is the research led by Professor Henry L. Roediger III and Jeffrey D. Karpicke from Washington University. Their study demonstrated that students who engaged in repetitive self-testing retained information significantly longer than those who merely studied the material or created a concept map.

This finding dovetails with the Retrieval Practice Effect, a well-established theory in cognitive psychology. According to this theory, the act of retrieving a memory strengthens the neural connections associated with it, making the memory easier to access in the future.

Analogously, in the train station of your mind, the more frequently a particular train — or memory — is called upon and retrieved, the more efficient the route to that train becomes. This active retrieval, facilitated by self-testing, acts as a signal booster, amplifying the signal strength each time the memory is recalled.

So, armed with the power of self-testing, you're effectively engaging in a scientifically-proven exercise regimen for your mind, enhancing your memory fitness, and equipping yourself for a more effective learning journey. The science is clear: To catch the right train of thought, make self-testing your trusty stationmaster.

Maximize Memory Consolidation

Picture your brain as a bustling city, with neurons as inhabitants communicating via synapses, much like roads connecting houses. When neurons frequently interact — or 'fire' — the synapses between them grow robust, akin to roads being widened and paved. This fortifying process, known as consolidation, can be enhanced through good sleep, which acts like a diligent city cleaner, sweeping away the toxins and paving way for better connections. Exercise, too, plays a crucial role. It acts like a visionary city planner, promoting neurogenesis, or the birth of new neurons, enhancing the city's growth and connectivity. A simple stroll outdoors can work wonders for your memory.

Practice Smart Studying

The effectiveness of studying is not determined by the duration but by the method. Studying non-stop for hours on end is akin to trying to sprint a marathon — you'll quickly burn out and remember less. Instead, studying in short, focused bursts with restful intervals, similar to high-intensity interval training, is the way to go. During the breaks, rewarding yourself with a simple pleasure — a walk, a cup of tea, or even a quick chore — will help switch your brain from focused to diffuse mode, optimizing the learning pathways.

Build Powerful Associations

The process of learning can be made easier by creating associations, much like using vivid color-coded labels in our metaphorical filing system. Analogies serve as these colorful tags, breaking down complex concepts into simpler, more relatable terms. Imagine your working memory as a versatile robot with four arms, busily weaving threads of new information into the fabric of long-term memory. Drawing on prior knowledge, akin to reviewing an existing design, can enhance your understanding and imbue you with the confidence to weave intricate, new patterns in your memory fabric.

Conclusion

By embracing these strategies, you can enhance your ability to learn, fine-tuning that master key and unlocking an even more remarkable world of knowledge.
