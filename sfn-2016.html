Modality Specific Neural Signals of Evidence Accumulation for Spatial Decisions

Published on: Nov 01, 2016

Introduction

Embarking on the journey of my PhD, my intrigue gravitated towards understanding the intricate process of how humans make decisions. This requires a remarkable ability to perceive, accumulate, and integrate information.

The Research Question

Given the existing theory that sensory information is processed in modality-specific sensory regions, under the influence of fronto-parietal cortices to form spatial decisions, I began to question - do these brain regions accumulate modality-specific sensory evidence, or evidence that directs an action?

The Methodology

In pursuit of answers, we designed a study with two principal objectives during my doctoral research. The study employed an event-related functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) technique on 17 healthy human subjects along with patients harboring lesions in parietal cortices. The chosen methodology was a spatial perceptual accumulation task, utilizing both visual and auditory modalities.

The Findings

My findings showed that regions correlating with the signed evidence were the occipital cortex for the visual task, and the superior temporal gyrus for the auditory task. In a fascinating turn, the fronto-parietal cortices didn't align with the signed evidence but with the absolute evidence in both modalities.

The Impact

Patients with unilateral parietal cortex lesions exhibited a performance decrease in both the visual and auditory tasks. This crucial observation suggests that sensory evidence appears to accumulate in modality-specific sensory cortices, while supramodal activity in parietal cortices might reflect evidence pointing towards an action choice.

Conclusion

The learnings from this study, presented as an abstract at the Society for Neuroscience (SfN) 2016 in San Diego, highlighted interesting facets of spatial decision-making. They continue to be instrumental in providing deeper insights into our understanding of the brain.
